Phase 1 · Before opening Lovable

Three Diagnostic Questions

Answer all three in writing before your team opens Lovable.
1
What is our riskiest assumption?
What would have to be true for this product to succeed, that you are least confident about right now?
2
What is the minimum we need to build to test that assumption?
The smallest thing that creates a real experience for a real user, not the full product.
3
Who is the user, and what is the one action they need to complete?
Name one action. If you have two, your team has not yet agreed on a focus.
Teams that skip this step tend to build more than they need and learn less. Write the answers down before you prompt Lovable.
Phase 1 · Writing your Lovable prompt

Prompt Structure and App Requirements

Use this template as a starting point. Be explicit about what to leave out.
"Build a [mobile / web] app for [user] who [pain point, one sentence].
The app must allow users to [core action].
Include at least 2-3 connected screens with working navigation.
Do not include [features you are deliberately leaving out — name them].
Design should feel [tone or style, e.g., clean, mobile-first, minimal]."
Your app should include A real user flow: screen 1 leads to screen 2 leads to a completed action. Navigation works. The core interaction is functional, not decorative.
What to avoid Lovable will add features by default. Name what you are leaving out in your prompt. A hero section with a non-functional button is not an MVP.
Lean principle Build only what tests your riskiest assumption. Every feature that does not help you learn something is waste (Ries, The Lean Startup, 2011).
Phase 2 · User testing

Testing Protocol

Each A team tests a B team's prototype. Each B team tests an A team's prototype. Two distinct roles during the session.
The Tester
  • Attempt the core task without being told anything about the app first
  • Say what you are thinking out loud as you navigate
  • When finished (or stuck), answer: "What did you think this was for when you first opened it?"
  • Then answer: "What would make you actually use this?"
The Builder (watching)
  • Do not explain anything before the tester starts
  • Do not prompt or coach while they navigate — confusion during testing is data
  • Record: where they hesitate, what they misread, what surprised you
  • Leave with: 1 thing understood immediately, 1 point of confusion, 1 unexpected reaction
The urge to explain your product while someone is struggling to use it is a signal that the design has a problem, not that the user does.
Phase 3 · Iteration

One Iteration: Identify, Decide, Build

Based on what you observed in Phase 2, make one targeted change to your prototype.
Step 1
Name what you learned
Write one sentence: "We learned that users [X]." If you cannot say it in one sentence, the team has not agreed on the finding.
→
Step 2
Choose the change
One change only. If the team disagrees about which change matters most, that disagreement is the prioritization exercise.
→
Step 3
Prompt Lovable
Describe the specific change. Specify what to keep unchanged. Be precise so Lovable does not rebuild the whole app.
You will want to fix several things. Choosing one and defending that choice is how product prioritization works in practice. Be ready to explain why this change over the others.
Debrief · 6 minutes

Discussion Questions

One 60-second demo per concept (show the iteration, not just the MVP), then three questions for the room.
1
What was your riskiest assumption going in, and did user testing support or challenge it?
2
What change did you make in Phase 3, and why that change over the others you could have made?
3
You completed one Build-Measure-Learn cycle in under an hour. What would you do differently at the start of the next one?
Connection to Sprint 3: your PRD requires a vibe-coded prototype with a deployed link. The decisions you made today — what to leave out, what to prioritize after feedback — are the same decisions that separate a useful prototype from an over-built one.

Phase 1 · Concept assignment
Group A

Campus Food Finder

Pain Point
Each dining location on campus has its own ordering system, or no system at all. Students have no centralized way to see what is available, which locations are busy, or where they can realistically get through quickly right now.
Job to Be Done
When I am short on time between classes, I want to find which campus dining option I can get through fastest right now and place an order for pickup, so I can eat without being late.
Product Idea

A centralized campus dining app showing all locations, real-time wait estimates or busyness levels, and the ability to pre-order for pickup from any location in one place.

Phase 1 · Concept assignment
Group B

Renter Credential Profile

Pain Point
Rental applications require the same sensitive documents uploaded repeatedly to unfamiliar third-party portals. The process is tedious, invasive, and duplicated each time a renter applies to a new property.
Job to Be Done
When I am applying for an apartment, I want to verify my financial reliability quickly and securely, so I can move forward without repeatedly uploading sensitive documents to unknown platforms.
Product Idea

A verified renter profile: upload and verify documents once, then share a credentialed summary with any landlord in one step, with control over what is disclosed.
